Pregnancy worries? Help is at hand.

January 05, 2023 10:53 AM

Pregnancy is a life-altering experience full of new emotions, challenges and responsibilities.

Pregnancy is an incredible experience. While most women welcome it with open arms, the prospect of motherhood can be daunting for others.

Pregnancy-related anxiety stems from several factors, including uncertainty about delivery, the baby’s well-being and limited health literacy.

Studies have shown that a pregnant woman's health literacy level correlates with her recovery care needs, which in turn influence pregnancy-related anxiety. If you are pregnant, it is important to know more about the health conditions that can affect you and the care that is necessary.

Maternal iron deficiency

Mothers-to-be generally experience a 20-30% increase in body blood volume, requiring more iron and vitamins to produce sufficient haemoglobin. The inability to do so can result in anaemia.

The types of anaemia that occur during pregnancy include iron-deficiency anaemia, folate-deficiency anaemia, and vitamin B12 deficiency anaemia.

While mild anaemia is common, it can worsen and put your child at greater risk of developing anaemia too during infancy. For the mother, it raises the likelihood of blood loss during delivery, and decreases the body's ability to fight off infections.

Rapid heartbeat, tiredness and shortness of breath are all signs of anaemia during pregnancy but may go unnoticed at first, being similar to general pregnancy symptoms.

Talk to your obstetrician regularly on how to maintain a healthy lifestyle and manage pregnancy symptoms.

Blood tests to screen for anaemia are a part of routine prenatal care, so it is crucial to see your obstetrician regularly regardless of whether or not you're experiencing any symptoms.

Eating iron-rich foods (such as red meat, peanuts and dark green leafy vegetables) can also help you keep a healthy level of iron in your system during pregnancy. Additionally, your midwife or obstetrician may recommend that you take vitamin and mineral supplements to increase your intake of iron and folic acid.

Declining blood circulation

Throughout pregnancy, your body and blood circulation take on the additional task of supplying oxygen and nourishment to the foetus. As your baby grows, your heart has to work harder to pump blood to sustain both you and the foetus. This can raise your heart rate and slow down blood circulation.

This may cause varicose veins - which are swollen, elevated veins in the legs or feet that indicate blood circulation problems.

To boost circulation, your obstetrician may recommend cardiovascular exercises on a stationary bike or treadmill for at least 30 minutes every day.

Regular moderate physical activity during pregnancy helps prepare your body for childbirth.

If you wish to continue working during pregnancy, activities that involve long periods of sitting or standing can be very uncomfortable. Compression stockings may be useful in these situations as they provide gentle pressure to ease discomfort and decrease swelling in the feet and ankles.

Consuming the right food or supplements containing tomato, turmeric and ginger is also recommended to improve your blood circulation.

However, you should never make significant changes to your daily routine during pregnancy without first discussing it with your obstetrician.

Stress urinary incontinence

Pregnancy and childbirth place an enormous strain on the body, both physically and emotionally. Stress urinary incontinence (SUI) is the most prevalent bladder issue during pregnancy that occurs due to sudden strain on the bladder and urethra.

SUI occurs when the pelvic floor becomes weaker due to fluctuating progesterone levels during pregnancy, and childbirth - especially vaginal delivery, also stretches and weakens the pelvic floor muscles.

Some women feel too ashamed to seek help, but it is a common condition and SUI is curable, with both surgical and non-surgical options readily available.

Always talk to your obstetrician or midwife about your pregnancy worries.

Two of the most common surgical procedures for SUI are mid-urethral and pubovaginal slings.

For non-surgical options, your obstetrician may recommend Kegel exercises to strengthen the pelvic floor muscles. Changes in lifestyle and diet - drinking less coffee and tea for instance, may also help reduce the severity of SUI.

If you have pregnancy-related anxiety, you do not need to suffer in silence. Voice your concerns, and be open to help. Always keep in mind that as a mother-to-be, you can have access to the support you need, both during and after childbirth to experience a more pleasant and fulfilling pregnancy.
