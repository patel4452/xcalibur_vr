Keeping an Eye on Your Eyes

June 30, 2022 10:53 AM

As you grow older, regular eye exams are essential as adults are prone to diseases that may cause blindness or other serious eye-related issues.

Our eyes are estimated to enable roughly 80% of our day-to-day activities. Imagine the dangers of driving should your vision be blurry, or the difficulties in working on a computer if you struggle to focus on the text on the screen.

Unfortunately, many people take their eyesight for granted or dismiss the seriousness of blurry vision and dark spots. It is crucial to act immediately to have your eyes checked so as to treat the symptoms or even stop the progress of more harmful eye diseases.

Here are a few tips to keep your eyes in good shape.

Choosing the appropriate eyewear

It is best to speak to a certified optometrist for the best eyewear for you.

Eyewear comes in various shapes and sizes and is typically used to correct vision caused by refractive errors such as myopia (near-sightedness), hyperopia (farsightedness), and astigmatism.

Single vision lenses are tailored to combat the aforementioned eye conditions.

Meanwhile, both bifocal and multifocal lenses (sometimes known as progressive lenses) correct a condition known as presbyopia. A common symptom of presbyopia is the degeneration in your eye’s ability to focus on objects nearby, and it becomes more prominent in people above the ages of 40 to 65.

Wearing glasses with the wrong prescriptions can cause substantial discomfort and eyestrain – resulting in fatigue, blurry vision, persistent headaches, and other negative symptoms. Therefore, it is important to work with a certified eye specialist to evaluate the right prescription and choose lenses that are most suited for your eyes.

On top of vision correction, the mileage you get from your eyewear can vary based on the lenses used – depending on the material, weight, and coating used. For example, due to its impact resistance, polycarbonate glasses are a great option if you are into sports or other activities that could damage your eyewear.

Here’s a list of popular types of lenses:

  • Polycarbonate
  • Trivex
  • Aspheric
  • High-index plastic

List of popular coatings:

  • Anti-reflective (AR) coating
  • Scratch-resistant coating
  • Anti-fog coating
  • Ultraviolet (UV) treatment
  • Photochromatic

Consult a medical professional

Prevention is always better than cure.

Discovering an eye condition early makes it much easier to treat and reduces the risk of further complications.

Undergo regular eye examinations based on your age. Young adults aged 20-39 should schedule a thorough eye exam once every two to three years, while adults above the age of 40 should take a comprehensive eye exam once a year.

It is also important to keep track of your medical history, including:

  • general medical record
  • previous eye conditions
  • family eye history
  • prescribed medication
  • allergies
  • social history

Sharing this information when consulting your doctor helps them determine the most suitable treatments based on your situation.

Giving your eyes some rest

Take a break from the constant glare of your phone

Given the sheer accessibility of digital devices today, our eyes are put under constant strain. We communicate using mobile phones, work on computer screens, and even handle essentials like groceries or banking via online services.

To help mitigate the strain on your eyes, first consider adjusting the brightness settings on all your devices. Aim to keep brightness levels on the lower side while still maintaining visibility.

Another tip that helps ease eye strain is the 20:20:20 rule. According to Optometry Times: for every 20 minutes of screen time, focus your eyes on something 20 feet (or roughly 6 metres) away for 20 seconds. This process helps you relax and refocus your eye muscles at regular intervals.

Lead a healthy lifestyle

An active lifestyle can help improve your vision

A journal published by the Survey of Ophthalmology suggests a link between obesity and a greater risk of several eye-health complications.

While the exact relationship between the two is still uncertain, it is hypothesised to be related to the narrowing of blood vessels associated with obesity. This would cause the blood vessels to face difficulties when supplying oxygen and other essential nutrients to the optical region.

As a general goal, the average person should aim for a minimum of 30 minutes of moderate physical activity daily with a mix of aerobic exercise and strength training.

As for a healthy diet, The Nutrition Source by Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health has the following recommendations:

1. Eating whole grain (brown rice, whole-wheat bread) over refined carbs (white bread, pastries)

2. Choose foods that contain healthy fats, such as plant oils, nuts, and fish.

3. Consume more vegetables and fruits into your diet. Add variety by including dark green, yellow, orange, and red vegetables.
